# Persian-LLM-Eval
> A culturally-grounded benchmark for evaluating Large Language Models on real-world Persian language - with a focus on social media language, cultural context, and fairness.  

## Motivation
Persian (Farsi) is spoken by over 130 million people across Iran, Afghanistan, and Tajikestan, yet it remains one of the most underrepresented languages in LLM evaluation research. Existing Persian benchmarks are largely built on formal text, academic literature, school exam questions, and translated English datasets, which fail to capture the informal, culturally-loaded language that dominates how Persian speakers actually communicate online.  
This project aims to fill that gap by building a benchmark grounded in **real-world Persoan social media language**, evaluating how well current LLMs handle the cultural nuance, informal register, and contextual knowledge that formal benchmarks systematically miss. Beyond evaluation, we conduct a **cultural bias analysis** to surface where and why models fail on Persian-specific content, contributing to the broader conversation on multilingual fairness in AI.  

## What This Benchmark Covers
* **Informal language understanding**, colloquial Persian as used on social media platforms, distinct from formal written Persian.
* **Cultural knowledge & context**, Iranian cultural references, idioms, humor, and social norms that require geniune cultural grounding to interpret correctly.
* **Sentiment * pragmatics**, sentiment analysis and pragmatic inference in culturally-specific contexts.
* **Cross-model evaluation**, systematic evaluation across major LLMs (GPT-40, Claude, Llama, Qwen, Mistral, and others)
* **Bias & fairness analysis**, identifying patterns in model failures that reveal cultural blind spots.

## Why This Matters
Current multilingual benchmarks share two critical limitations:  
1. They are predominately translated from English, inheriting Western cultural assumptions
2. They evaluate formal language, missing the register where most Persian speakers actually interact with AI systems

A model that scores well on translated academic benchmarks may still fail a Persian-speaking user asking a culturally-loaded questions in everyday language. This benchmark is designed to measure that gap.
